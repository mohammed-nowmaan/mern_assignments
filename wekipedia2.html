Mahatma Gandhi

Born 2 October 1869
Porbandar, Gujarat, India
Died 30 January 1948
New Delhi, India
Occupation Lawyer, Political Activist
Known for Nonviolent resistance,
Indian independence

Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi (2 October 1869 – 30 January 1948) was an Indian lawyer, anti-colonial nationalist, and political ethicist who employed nonviolent resistance to lead the successful campaign for India's independence from British rule.

Early Life and history

Gandhi was born in Porbandar, Gujarat, and went on to study law in London. He later practiced law in South Africa, where he experienced racial discrimination, which sparked his commitment to civil rights and nonviolent protest.

Parents

Gandhi's father, Karamchand Uttamchand Gandhi (1822–1885), served as the dewan (chief minister) of Porbandar state.[4][5] His family originated from the then village of Kutiana in what was then Junagadh State.[6] Although Karamchand only had been a clerk in the state administration and had an elementary education, he proved a capable chief minister.[6]

Philosophy and Movements

He developed the philosophy of Satyagraha, or nonviolent resistance, which he used to lead movements such as the Non-Cooperation Movement, the Salt March, and the Quit India Movement against British rule.

Key Events

  • 1893: Begins civil rights work in South Africa
  • 1915: Returns to India and joins Indian National Congress
  • 1930: Leads the Salt March to Dandi
  • 1942: Launches Quit India Movement
  • 1948: Assassinated in New Delhi

Legacy

Gandhi is remembered as the "Father of the Nation" in India. His teachings inspired civil rights movements around the world, including those led by Martin Luther King Jr. and Nelson Mandela.
